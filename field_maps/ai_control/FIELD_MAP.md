```fieldmap-about
This map reads the AI-control literature as a question ledger. AI control asks how monitoring, trusted/untrusted-model composition, editing, resampling and auditing can keep a deployment safe even when the untrusted model is adversarial and trying to subvert oversight. The ROOT axis RQ carries the field's research questions. The other axes are read per paper: PROTOCOL is the control technique studied (trusted monitoring, untrusted monitoring, defer-to-trusted, trusted editing, resampling, scalable oversight, or none); THREAT is the side task the untrusted model attempts (backdoor, exfiltration, sabotage, covert side-objective, collusion, research sabotage); SETTING is how realistic the environment is, from a toy APPS backdoor to a real codebase; MONITOR is the oversight channel (trusted action monitor, chain-of-thought monitor, white-box probe, human audit, or an ensemble); and GOAL is what the study is for (protocol design, attack capability, detection method, benchmark, measurement, or safety case). A cell is one full coordinate point; the papers there answer it, authored empty points are research gaps, and papers are weighted by recency and citations so the current front and the canon read heaviest. This map is complementary to the scheming map (a model's propensity to scheme) - control assumes adversariality and studies the protocol.
```

## §8 Main tensor findings

Objective observations from the assembled tensor (no recommendations):

- **The field is monitor-centric.** Of the six MONITOR values, the trusted action monitor and the chain-of-thought monitor carry most of the catalog; white-box probes (P043, P044) and ensemble monitors (P012, P015) are thin, and the internal-state probe results so far are negative or fragile.
- **Trusted monitoring dominates PROTOCOL coverage; trusted editing and resampling are sparse.** The founding paper (P001) still supplies most of the trusted-editing evidence, and resampling is anchored almost entirely by Ctrl-Z (P004).
- **Attack-selection is a recently-opened front.** P008, P019 and P020 converge on the same fact: measured safety is a function of how the red team selects attacks, not just of raw capability, so protocols that looked safe under a fixed attack are not.
- **Convergence cells (many future-work edges landing):** realistic multi-step agentic transfer (F1) and monitor hardening against adaptive attacks (F3) draw the most demand; white-box/hybrid detection (F4) and collusion bounds (F6) are the most-requested still-open directions.
- **Golden threads (cross-paper warnings):** (1) a monitor evaluated against a fixed attacker over-states safety; (2) reading the chain-of-thought helps only while it stays faithful, and training pressure, obfuscation and non-English reasoning all erode that faithfulness; (3) untrusted monitoring's safety rests on a collusion bound nobody can yet lower-bound from training dynamics.

## §9 Deep-dive on the central RQ

The spine question is RQ1 - which control protocol achieves the best safety-usefulness trade-off. Backbone works: P001 (trusted monitoring / editing / untrusted monitoring on APPS), P002 (the game-theoretic optimum), P003 (adaptive macro-protocols), P004 (resampling in agentic settings) and P026 (protocol evaluation in SHADE-Arena). Transferability: the toy APPS frontier (P001, P002) is only beginning to be reproduced in realistic agentic settings (P004, P026), and no catalogued paper measures head-to-head whether the toy protocol ranking survives the move to multi-file, long-horizon deployments (greenfield q035). Contradiction/tension: P002 computes an optimal protocol under a fixed attack model, while P008/P019/P020 show adaptive attack selection erodes exactly that guarantee - the field disputes how much a computed frontier means once the red team adapts. Control tax: measured (P001, P004) but not yet optimized as a primary objective (the F7 requests, q005).
